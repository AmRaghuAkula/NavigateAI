The NavigateAI Framework

The 3-Pillar Readiness Check

Before any AI tool goes live, three foundations must be solid. Not perfect — solid. Each pillar has a clear minimum standard that separates ready from not-yet-ready.

P1
Data Quality
Is your data clean enough to learn from?
AI systems produce outputs proportional to the quality of their inputs. Before connecting any tool to your data, confirm it meets a minimum usable standard — not perfect, but consistent, accessible, and representative.
The data exists and covers a meaningful time period
Key fields are populated for at least 80% of records
Obvious duplicates and outliers have been reviewed
A named person owns ongoing data quality for this source
P2
Data Accessibility
Can the right tool reach the right data?
Data that exists but can't be accessed by your AI tool is functionally invisible. Map the path from your data source to your tool — including permissions, APIs, and refresh frequency — before you start building.
Data source has a reliable export or API connection
Access permissions have been reviewed and approved
Refresh frequency matches the decision cadence you need
Sensitive fields have been identified and handled appropriately
P3
Security & Compliance
Is your organisation protected when this goes live?
Security and compliance are not afterthoughts. They define what data can be used, how it can flow, and what happens if something goes wrong. Confirm these before deployment — not after a customer or regulator asks.
Data privacy policy reviewed and updated for AI usage
Regulatory obligations mapped (GDPR, HIPAA, SOC 2, etc.)
Vendor security certifications confirmed and DPAs signed
AI training data clauses reviewed in vendor terms
The readiness test If a human can't find the latest version of your data reliably, neither can your AI. Accessibility is not a technical problem — it is an organisational one.
Know Where You Stand

The Data Trust Ladder

Before deploying AI, locate your data on this five-rung ladder. Your current rung determines your path forward — not a barrier, just an honest starting point with a clear next step.

1
Scattered
Data exists but is fragmented, inconsistent, and hard to access
Information lives across multiple tools with no integration. Quality is unknown. Ownership is unclear. Next step: identify your 2–3 most critical data sources and assign an owner to each.
2
Centralised
Data is in one or two accessible systems but quality is inconsistent
You know where your data lives. Quality has known issues — duplicates, gaps, stale records. Next step: run a focused data clean-up on the specific fields your AI use case depends on.
3
✦ Minimum Viable — most founders deploy from here
Data is accessible, reasonably clean, and owned
Key data sources are connected, quality is good enough for the specific use case, and someone owns the ongoing accuracy. This is the minimum standard for a responsible first deployment.
4
Governed
Data has documented ownership, policies, and regular quality reviews
Formal data governance is in place. Access controls are defined. Data quality is actively monitored. This is the standard for production AI at scale.
5
AI-Ready
Data infrastructure is built specifically to support AI workloads
Data pipelines are automated, quality is validated continuously, and the organisation has the capability to retrain and improve AI systems over time.
Stage 5 — Take Action

Confirm your foundations. Then proceed.

Use the checklist to confirm all three pillars are solid. The readiness worksheet helps you document your data state and security posture before your first deployment.

✦ You are ready to proceed when…
Your data is at Rung 3 or above on the Data Trust Ladder
All three pillars — Quality, Accessibility, Security — have been reviewed
A named data owner exists for each source your AI tool will use
Vendor security certifications confirmed and data processing terms signed
AI training data clauses reviewed — opt-out confirmed if required
Regulatory obligations mapped and compliance requirements met
✓ Common mistakes to avoid
Not waiting for perfect data — Rung 3 is sufficient to start
Not discovering data access issues for the first time mid-implementation
Not treating security review as optional because "we're small"
