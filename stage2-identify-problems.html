The NavigateAI Framework

The Problem Anatomy Tool

A strong AI problem statement passes four tests. Run every candidate problem through all four lenses before deciding it's worth pursuing. Weak problems fail at least one.

L1
Decision
What decision does this improve?
Every valuable AI problem sits behind a real decision someone needs to make better, faster, or more consistently. If you can't name the decision, you haven't found the problem yet.
"If AI solved this, who would make a different decision — and what would they decide differently?"
L2
Frequency
How often does this happen?
AI delivers the most value on problems that repeat. A task done once a year is not an AI candidate. A task done 200 times a week almost always is. Frequency is what turns a fix into a force multiplier.
"How many times per week does this occur — and what does each occurrence cost in time, money, or quality?"
L3
Data
Is there data to learn from?
AI systems learn from patterns in past examples. If the problem has never been documented, measured, or logged, there is nothing for AI to work with. Data availability is a hard prerequisite, not an afterthought.
"Does a record exist of how this has been handled before — and is that record accessible and consistent?"
L4
Impact
Does solving it actually matter?
Not every inefficiency is worth fixing with AI. The best problems have a clear downstream impact — on revenue, customer experience, employee time, or risk. If solving it doesn't visibly move a metric, reconsider.
"If this problem were solved tomorrow, what would get measurably better — and how would you know?"
The rule of thumb A strong AI problem is one you can describe in a single sentence: who has the problem, what they're doing instead, how often, and what it costs. If it takes a paragraph, keep digging.
Match Problem to AI Type

The Four Problem Classes

Not all problems need the same kind of AI. Matching the problem class to the right AI approach is what separates fast wins from expensive experiments. Identify your class before you look at tools.

⚙️
Automation
Repetitive Tasks
The same structured steps, repeated at volume. Human involvement adds no judgment value — only time and cost.
"We manually copy invoice data from email into our accounting system — 50 invoices a day."
Ask: Could this be done without any human thinking — just following a consistent rule or pattern?
🔭
Insight
Slow or Blind Decisions
Decisions that are delayed, inconsistent, or risky because the right information isn't surfaced at the right time.
"We make inventory decisions on Mondays using data that's already 5 days old."
Ask: Is a decision being made slower or worse than it should be because someone lacks timely, accurate information?
✍️
Creation
Repetitive Content Work
Writing, summarising, translating, or formatting the same types of content repeatedly — where structure is consistent but data changes.
"Every proposal has the same sections. Only the client details and pricing change."
Ask: Is a human producing content that follows a predictable structure most of the time — just with different inputs?
🔗
Coordination
Multi-Step Workflows
Complex processes that span multiple tools, people, or systems — where handoffs are manual, slow, or prone to something falling through the cracks.
"Onboarding a new client touches 4 systems and 3 people across 2 weeks. Steps get missed."
Ask: Is something getting dropped, delayed, or done inconsistently because coordination depends on human memory?
Stage 2 — Take Action

Find your problems. Frame them properly.

Use the checklist to confirm you've identified the right candidates, then use the Problem Statement Builder to write them up clearly — one per problem, ready to take into Stage 3.

✦ Your problem is ready when…
You can describe it in one sentence — who, what, how often, what it costs
You've named the decision it improves — not just the task it replaces
It happens frequently enough that solving it compounds over time
There is existing data or a clear path to create it
You know which of the four problem classes it belongs to
Solving it moves a metric your business already cares about
✓ Common mistakes to avoid
Not chasing a problem because a tool exists for it
Not confusing symptoms ("slow reporting") for root problems
Not selecting problems that only happen occasionally
