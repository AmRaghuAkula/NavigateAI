The NavigateAI Framework

The AI Tool Compass

Every problem from Stage 2 belongs to one of four classes. Each class maps to a different type of AI tool. Use this compass to identify the right tool category before you look at a single vendor.

From Stage 2: Repetitive Tasks → Automation Tools
⚙️
Workflow & Process Automation
For structured, rule-based tasks that repeat at volume. The AI follows a defined sequence — triggering, extracting, routing, or updating — without human involvement at each step.
What to look for
No-code or low-code workflow builders
Integration platforms that connect your existing tools
Document processing and data extraction tools
Robotic process automation for legacy system tasks
From Stage 2: Slow Decisions → Intelligence Tools
🔭
Analytics & Insight Platforms
For decisions that are delayed or inconsistent because data isn't surfaced at the right time. These tools ingest your operational data and return predictions, anomalies, or recommendations on demand.
What to look for
Dashboards that surface leading indicators, not just lag metrics
Predictive models built on your CRM or operational data
Anomaly detection for inventory, churn, or financial patterns
Natural language query tools that let non-technical staff interrogate data
From Stage 2: Repetitive Content → Generation Tools
✍️
Content & Knowledge Tools
For writing, summarising, translating, or formatting content that follows a predictable structure. These tools are trained on your brand voice, templates, and knowledge base to produce consistent output at scale.
What to look for
Large language model tools with custom instructions or system prompts
Document generation tools that merge templates with live data
Knowledge base tools that surface internal content on demand
Email and communication assistants trained on your tone and style
From Stage 2: Multi-Step Workflows → Orchestration Tools
🔗
Agent & Orchestration Platforms
For complex, multi-step workflows that cross systems, tools, and people. These platforms coordinate sequences of AI actions — including triggering other tools, making conditional decisions, and managing handoffs.
What to look for
Agent platforms that can interact with your existing tools via API
Workflow orchestration tools with conditional logic and error handling
Human-in-the-loop capabilities for steps that still need oversight
Audit trails and monitoring for complex automated processes
The navigation rule Match your problem class first. Then evaluate tools within that class. Never start with a tool and work backwards to justify it.
A Decision Every Founder Faces

Build it or buy it?

For most small organisations, the answer is almost always to buy first. But knowing when building is worth the investment — and what "building" actually means in 2026 — prevents both costly custom builds and missed competitive advantages.

Consider building when…
Building your own
Configuring, fine-tuning, or assembling AI capabilities using existing platforms and APIs — not writing models from scratch. "Build" in 2026 means customisation, not creation.
Makes sense when:
The workflow is highly specific to how you operate and no off-the-shelf tool maps to it
Your proprietary data is the competitive advantage — and you need it to stay internal
You have technical capability and the problem justifies a longer investment horizon
You've already validated the use case with an off-the-shelf tool and hit its ceiling
Warning: don't build what you can buy. Custom builds take 3–5× longer than founders expect and require ongoing maintenance that rarely gets resourced.
✦ Start here for most problems
Buying off-the-shelf
Deploying a purpose-built AI tool designed for your problem class. Faster time-to-value, lower risk, and proven functionality — with configuration rather than construction.
Makes sense when:
The problem class is standard — customer support, document processing, reporting, scheduling
You need results within 90 days, not 9 months
You want to validate whether AI actually solves this problem before investing further
Your team lacks dedicated technical resource for ongoing development and maintenance
90% of enterprises now test third-party applications before building internally — even for use cases that initially seemed like obvious build candidates.
Before You Sign Anything

The 5 Vendor Evaluation Questions

Once you've identified your tool class and shortlisted 2–3 vendors, these are the five questions that separate good evaluations from expensive regrets.

01
Does it actually solve your specific problem — not just the general category?
Run a proof of concept on your real data and your real workflow before committing. Generic demos look impressive. Your actual use case is the only test that matters.
02
What happens to your data — and does the vendor train on it?
Check the data processing terms specifically for AI training clauses, data retention, and deletion rights. Many standard-tier products include opt-in training by default. Ask explicitly — and get the answer in writing.
03
Does it have the security certifications your organisation and industry require?
For most businesses: SOC 2 Type II as a baseline. If you handle health data: HIPAA BAA. If you have EU customers: GDPR Data Processing Agreement. If you handle payments: PCI-DSS. Missing the right certification means starting the vendor search over.
04
What does it cost to run this — not just to license it?
Account for the full cost: licence fees, usage-based API costs at your projected volume, integration time, and ongoing maintenance. AI tools often have usage costs that compound quickly at scale. Model the cost at 3× your expected volume before signing.
05
What does getting out look like — and what do you leave behind?
Understand data portability, contract exit terms, and what happens to any workflows, prompts, or configurations you've built inside the platform. Vendor lock-in is real in AI tooling. Knowing the exit cost upfront changes how aggressively you commit to configuration.
Stage 4 — Take Action

Shortlist your tools. Evaluate them honestly.

Use the checklist to confirm your tool selection is grounded. Then use the Vendor Scorecard to run a structured evaluation across your shortlist.

✦ Your tool selection is solid when…
You've identified the correct tool class from the AI Tool Compass before looking at vendors
You have 2–3 shortlisted tools to evaluate — not 10
You've run a proof of concept on real data — not just a vendor demo
Data training clauses, retention, and deletion rights have been reviewed
Required security certifications have been confirmed
Full cost of ownership — including usage at scale — has been modelled
✓ Traps to avoid
Not selecting a tool because it's well-known rather than right for your problem
Not building custom before validating that no off-the-shelf option works
Not signing a multi-year contract without understanding the exit terms
